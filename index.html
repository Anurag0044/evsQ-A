EVS – Environmental Science Q&A

Complete Guide with Diagrams and Explanations

1. What is an ecosystem?
An ecosystem is a community of living organisms (biotic components) in conjunction with the nonliving components (abiotic components) of their environment, interacting as a system. These biotic and abiotic components are linked together through nutrient cycles and energy flows.
Ecosystems can be of any size but usually encompass specific, limited spaces. They are controlled by external and internal factors. External factors such as climate, parent material, and topography control the overall structure of an ecosystem but are not themselves influenced by the ecosystem.

3. What are the functions of ecosystem?
Ecosystems perform several vital functions:
  • Energy Flow: Energy flows from the sun to producers and then to consumers in a unidirectional manner.
  • Nutrient Cycling: Nutrients are circulated between biotic and abiotic components through biogeochemical cycles.
  • Ecological Succession: The process by which the structure of a biological community evolves over time.
  • Homeostasis: Ecosystems maintain a balance between various components through self-regulation.
  • Productivity: Ecosystems produce biomass through primary and secondary production.
  • Decomposition: Breakdown of organic matter into simpler substances that can be reused.

6. Describe Bio-factory and its types.
A bio-factory is a facility that uses biological systems (microorganisms, plant or animal cells) to produce commercially valuable products through biotechnological processes.
Microbial Bio-factories

Use bacteria, yeast, or fungi to produce compounds like antibiotics, enzymes, and organic acids.

Plant Bio-factories

Use plant cell cultures to produce pharmaceuticals, flavors, and fragrances.

Animal Cell Bio-factories

Use mammalian cells to produce complex proteins, vaccines, and antibodies.

Algal Bio-factories

Use microalgae to produce biofuels, nutraceuticals, and pigments.

8. How does the energy flow in the ecosystem?
Energy flows through an ecosystem in a unidirectional manner, from the sun to producers and then to various levels of consumers. This flow follows the 10% law, where only about 10% of energy is transferred from one trophic level to the next.
The process involves:
  • Solar energy is captured by producers (plants) through photosynthesis
  • Primary consumers (herbivores) eat producers and obtain energy
  • Secondary consumers (carnivores) eat primary consumers
  • Tertiary consumers eat secondary consumers
  • Decomposers break down dead organisms, returning nutrients to the ecosystem
9. Define food web.
A food web is a complex network of interconnected food chains in an ecosystem. It represents the multiple feeding relationships between organisms, showing how energy and nutrients move through the ecosystem.
Unlike a food chain which is linear, a food web consists of many food chains that are linked together, providing a more realistic representation of feeding relationships in nature. This complexity makes ecosystems more stable, as the extinction of one species doesn't necessarily collapse the entire system.
10. What is forest ecosystem and its types?
A forest ecosystem is a natural woodland unit consisting of all plants, animals, and microorganisms in that area functioning together with all the non-living physical factors of the environment.
Tropical Rainforests

Located near the equator with high rainfall, temperature, and biodiversity.

Temperate Forests

Found in temperate zones with distinct seasons and moderate climate.

Boreal Forests (Taiga)

Coniferous forests in cold subarctic regions with long winters.

Deciduous Forests

Trees shed leaves annually; found in temperate regions.

11. What makes a good ecosystem?
A healthy, well-functioning ecosystem typically has:
  • High Biodiversity: Variety of species at all trophic levels
  • Efficient Energy Flow: Smooth transfer of energy through food chains
  • Nutrient Cycling: Effective recycling of essential elements
  • Resilience: Ability to withstand and recover from disturbances
  • Productivity: Adequate primary production to support the ecosystem
  • Balance: Proper predator-prey relationships and population control
  • Habitat Diversity: Variety of microhabitats supporting different species
12. What is ecological pyramid?
An ecological pyramid is a graphical representation designed to show the relationship between different organisms in an ecosystem, typically in terms of biomass, energy, or number of individuals at each trophic level.
Ecological Pyramid (Energy)
Tertiary Consumers (1 kcal/m²/year)

Types of ecological pyramids:
  • Pyramid of Energy: Shows energy content at each trophic level
  • Pyramid of Biomass: Shows total dry weight of organisms at each level
  • Pyramid of Numbers: Shows the number of individuals at each level
13. What are the functions and structure of forest ecosystem?
Structure of Forest Ecosystem:
  • Biotic Components: Trees, shrubs, herbs, animals, microorganisms
  • Abiotic Components: Soil, water, air, temperature, sunlight
  • Stratification: Canopy, understory, shrub layer, herb layer, forest floor
Functions of Forest Ecosystem:
  • Regulates climate and water cycles
  • Produces oxygen and sequesters carbon
  • Provides habitat for numerous species
  • Maintains soil fertility and prevents erosion
  • Offers recreational and aesthetic values
  • Provides timber, fuel, and other forest products
14. Define desert ecosystem and its types.
A desert ecosystem is a community of organisms and their environment in areas receiving less than 25 cm of rainfall per year, characterized by specialized plants and animals adapted to extreme conditions.
Hot and Dry Deserts

Extreme temperatures with little rainfall (e.g., Sahara, Sonoran)

Semiarid Deserts

Cooler than hot deserts with long dry seasons (e.g., Utah, Montana)

Coastal Deserts

Cool winters with moderate rainfall (e.g., Atacama, Namib)

Cold Deserts

Low temperatures with snow (e.g., Gobi, Antarctica)

15. What are the functions and structure of desert ecosystem?
Structure of Desert Ecosystem:
  • Biotic Components: Cacti, succulents, shrubs, reptiles, insects, small mammals
  • Abiotic Components: Sandy or rocky soil, minimal water, high temperature variations
  • Specialized Adaptations: Water conservation mechanisms, nocturnal behavior
Functions of Desert Ecosystem:
  • Mineral storage in arid soils
  • Habitat for specialized flora and fauna
  • Regulation of atmospheric composition
  • Source of unique genetic resources
  • Tourism and recreation opportunities
  • Potential for solar energy generation
16. Define aquatic ecosystem and its types.
An aquatic ecosystem is a water-based environment where living organisms interact with the physical and chemical features of their aquatic surroundings.
Freshwater Ecosystems
  • Lentic (still water): Lakes, ponds, wetlands
  • Lotic (flowing water): Rivers, streams
Marine Ecosystems
  • Oceans
  • Estuaries
  • Coral reefs
  • Mangrove forests
17. Define the functions and types of aquatic ecosystem.
Functions of Aquatic Ecosystems:
  • Regulation of global climate and water cycle
  • Habitat for diverse aquatic species
  • Water purification and waste treatment
  • Nutrient cycling and primary production
  • Recreation, tourism, and cultural values
  • Source of food and economic resources
Types of Aquatic Ecosystems:
  • Freshwater: Rivers, lakes, ponds, wetlands
  • Marine: Oceans, coral reefs, estuaries
  • Brackish: Mix of freshwater and saltwater
18. Define biosphere, lithosphere, and hydrosphere.
  • Biosphere: The global ecological system integrating all living beings and their relationships, including their interaction with the elements of the lithosphere, hydrosphere, and atmosphere.
  • Lithosphere: The rigid outer part of the Earth, consisting of the crust and upper mantle. It provides the solid surface for ecosystems.
  • Hydrosphere: The combined mass of water found on, under, and above the surface of a planet. It includes oceans, seas, lakes, rivers, groundwater, and atmospheric water vapor.
19. What is biodiversity and its major issues?
Biodiversity refers to the variety of life on Earth at all its levels, from genes to ecosystems, and the ecological and evolutionary processes that sustain it.
Major Issues Threatening Biodiversity:
  • Habitat Loss and Fragmentation: Due to agriculture, urbanization, deforestation
  • Climate Change: Altering habitats and species distributions
  • Pollution: Air, water, and soil pollution affecting species survival
  • Overexploitation: Overhunting, overfishing, illegal wildlife trade
  • Invasive Species: Non-native species outcompeting native species
  • Disease: Emerging diseases affecting wildlife populations
20. Define biosphere reserves.
Biosphere reserves are areas of terrestrial and coastal ecosystems which are internationally recognized within the framework of UNESCO's Man and the Biosphere (MAB) Programme. They promote solutions reconciling the conservation of biodiversity with its sustainable use.
Biosphere reserves have three interconnected functions:
  • Conservation: Protecting genetic resources, species, ecosystems, and landscapes
  • Development: Fostering economic and human development that is socio-culturally and ecologically sustainable
  • Logistic Support: Supporting research, monitoring, education, and information exchange
